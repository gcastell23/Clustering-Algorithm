Project Aim

Tracking Cellular Identity Transitions

A fundamental rule of biology is that distinct cell types exist in stable, specialized communities[cite: 2]. However, complex genetic diseases, such as HNF1A-driven monogenic diabetes, challenge this model[cite: 1]. Mutations do not instantly kill islet cells; instead, they slowly alter their genetic activity, pushing healthy cells into unstable, transitioning phenotypes[cite: 1].

Traditional clustering methods (such as PCA followed by Leiden or K-Means) use a flat Euclidean ruler, drawing rigid, geometric partitions[cite: 1, 2]. This flattens important biological variation and mislabels cells that fall along transition zones[cite: 1].

Our Solution: We built a noise-aware Information-Geometric framework designed to calculate transcriptomic distance based on expression variance rather than raw physical distance, allowing us to accurately track cell states dynamically[cite: 1].

Clinical Context

HNF1A-MODY serves as our primary test case. Because mutated beta cells gradually lose their identity rather than forming a distinct separate group, standard models struggle to resolve their location[cite: 1].

By deploying our mathematical model, we can trace this transition state as a continuous trajectory, rather than forcing cells into clean but incorrect classifications[cite: 1].

Algorithmic Innovation

Historical Breakthrough

Macosko et al. (2015)

Prior to droplet-based sequencing, researchers were forced to run bulk tissue arrays, averaging out expression profiles over thousands of combined cells[cite: 2]. By encapsulating single cells into oil droplets, Macosko and their team made it possible to read unique, individual cell transcriptomes[cite: 2]. This development established the foundation for mapping high-resolution cellular atlases[cite: 2].

Technical Challenges

FIRM Integration (2022)

Combining multi-center datasets introduces systematic experimental noise (batch effects) where cells mistakenly group by lab platform rather than biological type[cite: 1, 2]. As studied by Ming et al. (2022) with FIRM, aligning cell types across different platforms requires careful normalization to maintain underlying biological structures[cite: 1, 2].

Engineering Diary

How We Diagnosed and Fixed a Broken Algorithm

In our initial implementation of the IGMC pipeline, we observed a catastrophic failure: our model scored an Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) of just 0.076. It was practically performing random classification and was easily beaten by the standard baseline pipeline.

❌ The Bug: Naive Transition Matrix Argmax

Our original clustering logic mapped random walk probabilities onto a transition matrix $P_t$, then ran:

predicted_clusters = np.argmax(P_t, axis=1)

Why it failed: Identifying clusters using a local, cell-by-cell maximum index created thousands of tiny, disjointed micro-clusters[cite: 1]. It lacked global network awareness, ruining our ARI and failing to group matching cells together.

✅ The Fix: Spectral Graph Partitioning

We pivoted our approach. Instead of calculating a local maximum, we applied Spectral Clustering directly to our Information-Geometric Similarity Matrix ($W$)[cite: 1]:

spectral = SpectralClustering(affinity='precomputed')

Why it succeeded: Spectral clustering treats the cell network as a global graph, analyzing its eigenvalues to partition the data cleanly along true biological boundaries[cite: 1]. This structural correction immediately restored the model's performance.

Model Benchmark Comparison

By moving to our spectral similarity model, our clustering metric (ARI) outperformed standard Euclidean K-Means pipelines, while preserving fine-grained details for rare cell recovery[cite: 1].

Key Findings

1. Standard Pipelines Flay Boundaries:
Classic K-Means relies heavily on Euclidean distance, causing cells in transition zones to get mislabeled or grouped into incorrect categories[cite: 1, 2].

2. Info-Geometry Preserves Rare Cells:
Because our smart distance metric scales differences inversely to noise, low-expression markers that distinguish rare cells are preserved instead of being lost[cite: 1].

3. Conformal Sets Flag Uncertainty:
Instead of outputting an overconfident, single label, our conformal boundaries flag transitioning cells, enabling researchers to isolate shifting states dynamically[cite: 1].

Verdict

Our corrected model successfully outperforms baseline pipelines in both clustering accuracy and rare population identification[cite: 1].
